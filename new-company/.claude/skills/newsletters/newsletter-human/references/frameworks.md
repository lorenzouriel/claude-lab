## 1. Awareness levels (define the focus)

How much the reader already knows about you, the product/idea, their problem, and available solutions dictates **what** and **how** you write. The more aware of the pain, the more direct the approach can be.

- **Knows the product and knows it solves the need** → focus on the **PRODUCT** (offers, discounts, proof). Direct approach.
- **Doesn't know the product, but desires the solution** → focus on the **DESIRE**.
- **Doesn't know what they need, only feels a general problem** → focus on the **PROBLEM** and its solution.

Use a **subtle and indirect** approach (not evasive, not boring, not slow to offer) when: there is no trust in the results, the reader doesn't believe the problem has a solution, or they don't see the need to solve it.

## 2. The 5 opening types / leads

Choose by awareness level. You have ~100–600 characters to generate the emotional response after the headline has captured attention.

**1. Direct offer** — There is already awareness of you and the product; go straight to the offer (product, price, discount, bonus, scarcity, guarantee). Focus on the most emotionally appealing detail, reinforce the biggest benefit, and at some point give the *reason for the offer* to kill the "too good to be true" feeling. Ideal for simple products and buyers who already know you. Keep it short; use urgency and guarantee; be careful with too many bonuses (gives the impression that the product is weak).

**2. Promise that works** — Less direct; the product doesn't appear upfront. The big promise opens in the headline, the first line, and the last line. Works with those who are already aware of the problem and need a push. Start with the biggest benefit (speed, size, or quality of the result), know what has already been promised so you don't make a weak promise, and bring clear proof. How important is what the product does — or even more — is *how it makes the reader feel about themselves* and how they will be seen by others.

**3. Problem solution** — Subtle writing, driven by empathy. Show that you know what the reader's biggest pain is and that you understand how they feel. Target the pains that keep them up at night — but choose someone in a discomfort zone, not in an extreme situation (someone who is drowning rarely buys). Show the emotions first (proof of empathy), don't spend too long on the problem, and offer hope of a relevant solution.

**4. The big secret** — Grab attention with a secret only you know: it can be the solution or a hidden problem the reader hasn't identified. Give it a proper and intriguing name. Present the name in the headline, but only reveal it after the reader has read everything. Break skepticism with maximum specificity, and leave clues throughout the text. To create it: find the secret in the product, or take a benefit and create an analogy + proper name to make it seem new and secret.

**5. Story** — The most powerful of the five. Always contains a promise, but an indirect one. It must have: a powerful idea, a desirable benefit, an emotion, and an inevitable solution. It works because it makes information easy to absorb and remember, and because it destroys objections imperceptibly — the reader is emotionally engaged with the narrative, not feeling "convinced." (Every reader wants to buy, but nobody wants to feel they are being convinced.)

## 3. The 6 headline elements (with word banks)

Combine some (not all), keeping logical sense. "Exclusivity" emerges precisely from combining several elements. Appeal to the reader's self-interest and include the promise.

**1. Immediacy / urgency** — captures attention, sharpens curiosity.
Word bank: Discover · Now · Today · The Secret · The truth · Nobody ever told you · Revealed · Right now · Starting now.
Ex.: "Discover right now the shocking truth about…"

**2. Connection** — brings you closer to the reader; they feel capable.
Word bank: You · Ordinary people · Step by step · Starting from zero · Even if you've never · How I did it and you can too.
Ex.: "Discover the #1 secret that ordinary people use to…"

**3. Specificity** — deadline or number of steps. One of the most powerful.
Word bank: In X hours/days/weeks · X foolproof tips · X essential factors · X pillars.
Ex.: "7 steps to radically change your life in 30 days or less."

**4. Transformation** — the dream/goal your content delivers. Make the main goal very clear.
Ex.: "Personal trainer reveals the secret to losing belly fat in 8 weeks."

**5. Call to action** — ask for immediate action.
Word bank: Click here · Start now · Sign up and discover · Access now · Watch the video now · I want to see now.

**6. Exclusivity** — feeling of something unique, different from everything. Not an isolated term, but the sum of the others.
Compare: "How to change your life" (weak) vs. "7 steps to radically change your life in 30 days or less" (exclusive).

For SEO/blog titles: be direct about the subject, include the keyword, and try not to exceed ~55 characters so it doesn't get cut in search results.

## 4. The 6 basic emotions

Choose **one** dominant emotion. Not sure which? Start here:

1. **Disgust** — repulsion; protects from something harmful.
2. **Fear** — triggers flight or fight.
3. **Anger** — arises from frustration, injustice, or hostility.
4. **Surprise** — activates attention and curiosity; turns into anger or joy depending on the stimulus.
5. **Joy** — pleasure/happiness; initiates actions.
6. **Sadness** — reduces activity; **avoid**, as it does not lead to action.

Practical rule: people either want to **avoid a pain** or have a **strong desire** — choose one polarity and go deep. Negative emotions (anger, fear) tend to generate more action, which is why they are common — but if the reader is upset, they feel alone and misunderstood; break that feeling. People, deep down, want to be heroes for their families and friends — offer a solution that makes the reader the savior.

**Emotions the text must NEVER cause: confusion, boredom, doubt.** A bored or confused reader quits; doubt ("too good to be true") kills conversion.

## 5. Structure frameworks

### AIDA
- **Attention** — spark interest.
- **Interest** — show that you understand and solve the problem; personalize without overwhelming.
- **Desire** — emphasize benefits and results; use cases, proof, and testimonials.
- **Action** — clear and single CTA; no "tell me what you think."

### PAS
- **Problem** — a problem you and the reader share (builds trust).
- **Agitation** — make the reader feel the frustration; show the cost of not solving it.
- **Solution** — specific actions to resolve it.

### PASTOR (great for longer proposals/offers)
- **Problem** — name the problem in the reader's own language.
- **Amplify** — amplify the *cost of not solving it* (the pain of loss weighs more than gain — Prospect Theory). Quantify if possible.
- **Story/Solution** — tell the story of how you solve it (story activates more of the brain than a list of facts).
- **Transformation** — remember you are selling the *transformation*, not the service; prove it with testimonials/references that answer: "can they do it? have they done it for others? are they the right person?".
- **Offer** — describe the offer tied to the transformation and benefits. Honest offers, without exaggeration.
- **Response** — ask for the action. Assume the purchase and give clear instructions on how to proceed, reminding them why it matters.

### SUCCES (for ideas that "stick" — Made to Stick)
- **S**imple · **U**nexpected · **C**oncrete · **C**redible · **E**motional · **S**tories.
Storytelling tip: swap "I did this, and then that, and then that" for **"I did this, BUT… THEN…"** — it holds attention better and flows more naturally.

## 6. Cialdini's triggers ("Influence")

Use sparingly — not all at once:

- **Reciprocity** — deliver value for free first; humans are programmed to give back.
- **Social proof / social approval** — testimonials and cases increase credibility; we are influenced by others.
- **Liking / affinity** — we connect with those who are like us or have lived what we lived.
- **Authority** — we respect those we judge as "superior" on the subject; specialize and use good references.
- **Commitment and consistency** — those who commit publicly feel pressure to follow through; consistent text, without absurd promises.
- **Scarcity** — we value what we are about to lose; use real deadlines and limits.
