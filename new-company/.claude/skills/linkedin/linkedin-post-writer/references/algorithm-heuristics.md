# Algorithm heuristics

The 2026 posting rules this skill enforces while drafting. These are the
playbook's working assumptions about how LinkedIn distributes long-form text
posts. They are heuristics, not platform documentation. When a rule and the
user's explicit instruction collide, follow the user and note the tradeoff.

## The fold and the hook

- LinkedIn truncates the post at roughly **210 characters** on desktop before
  the "… see more" link. The hook has to do its full job inside that window.
- The first line carries the click. Lead with the strongest idea, not a wind-up.
  Inverted pyramid: insight first, context after.
- The hook should resolve in about **three lines**. If the reader has to work to
  understand why they should keep reading, they won't.
- No all-caps opener ("THIS CHANGED EVERYTHING"). It reads as bait and the scrub
  list rejects it.
- No opening rhetorical question. On LinkedIn it pattern-matches to AI-written
  filler and depresses dwell time.

## Length

- **Sweet spot: 900 to 1,300 characters** for a text post. This is the default
  target unless the user asked for short or long.
- Bands the skill offers: short 300-500, medium 900-1,300, long 1,500-1,900.
- The dwell-time playbook favors **300-400 words across 20+ short sentences**.
  Read that as: enough substance to hold someone for 20-plus seconds, never
  padded to hit a count.
- If a draft runs past 1,900 characters, cut, don't continue. Length without
  payoff reads as a wall and people bounce.

## Formatting

- **Double line-breaks between ideas, never single.** Single-spaced text
  collapses into a block on mobile and kills scannability.
- Short paragraphs. One to three lines each. Vary them so the shape is uneven.
- Use line breaks, occasional **bold**, and lists for scannability and dwell.
  Don't bold so much that nothing stands out.
- Vary sentence length hard. Mix a 3-word sentence against a 25-word one. Uniform
  rhythm is the loudest AI tell there is.
- Make at least one element save-worthy: a framework, a template, a specific
  number someone would screenshot.

## Hashtags and links

- **0 to 2 hashtags, placed at the very end.** More than two looks like reach
  begging and does little.
- **No external links in the body.** Outbound links in the post itself suppress
  reach. Put the link in the **first comment** and reference it ("link in the
  comments").
- If the post needs a destination, write the body to stand on its own first, then
  add the link below it as a comment after publishing.

## Timing

- Best windows: **Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday, 7:30 to 9:00 AM the reader's
  local time.** This is the default posting window the approval card suggests.
- Post at the **same days and times** each week. Consistency trains the audience
  to expect you, which lifts early engagement.
- Avoid Friday afternoon through Sunday for business content unless the audience
  is unusual and the user has data saying otherwise.

## Frequency

- **2 to 3 posts per week, maximum.** More than that splits your own reach and
  trains the algorithm to show each post to fewer people.
- Same slots every week beats sporadic bursts.

## The engagement protocol

The first 90 minutes decide a post's distribution. Treat publishing as the start
of the work, not the end.

- **Warm the room.** Spend 15 to 30 minutes engaging on others' posts right
  **before** publishing. This can lift reach by up to 20%.
- **Stay in the room.** Spend another 15 to 30 minutes engaging right **after**.
- **Reply to every comment inside the first hour.** Early replies pull the post
  back into feeds. The first 90 minutes set its distribution fate.
- **Be present in the feed daily.** 10 to 15 substantive comments on other
  people's posts per day. Substantive means a real sentence, not "great post."

## Dwell-time levers

These raise the time people spend on the post, which is the signal that matters
most after the early comment burst.

- Lead with the payoff so the reader commits, then make them stay for the detail.
- Include at least one real failure or behind-the-scenes moment. Failure posts
  draw around **8.5x** the engagement of polished wins.
- Write from first-hand experience. It validates expertise far better than
  marketing language and reads as human.
- End with a **genuine question**, one you'd actually want answered. Not "What do
  you think?" and not "tag someone who needs this." Engagement bait closers are
  on the scrub list.

## Quick reference

| Lever | Rule |
|---|---|
| Hook | Strongest idea in first 210 chars, resolves in 3 lines |
| Length | 900-1,300 chars default; 1,900 hard ceiling |
| Line breaks | Double between ideas, never single |
| Hashtags | 0-2, at the end |
| Links | None in body; first comment only |
| Best days | Tue / Wed / Thu |
| Best time | 7:30-9:00 AM reader's local time |
| Frequency | 2-3x per week, same slots |
| Before posting | 15-30 min engaging on others |
| After posting | 15-30 min engaging; reply to all comments in first hour |
| Daily | 10-15 substantive comments on others' posts |
| Must include | One failure or behind-the-scenes beat (8.5x lever) |
| Closer | One genuine question, no bait |
