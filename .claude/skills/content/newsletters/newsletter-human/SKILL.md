# Human Newsletter

A system for writing long-form content that grabs attention, delivers real value, and — above all — sounds like a person, not a machine. Distilled from classic copywriting frameworks and a practical content creation system for social networks.

## The rule that governs everything

Before a single line, fix three things. All the text serves them:

- **One idea.** A single central concept. The more points the text tries to cover, the weaker it gets. The temptation to solve several problems at once is mistake #1 — resist it.
- **One emotion.** One dominant emotion, chosen on purpose. Several emotions at once only cause confusion.
- **One action.** What the reader does when they finish. Everything leads to this.

And the backdrop of everything: **attention is the currency of the network.** The reader is giving you the scarcest thing they have — time. Maximize the value/attention ratio in every paragraph.

## The authenticity shortcut

The fastest way to sound human is to **write for your "past self"** — someone 1 or 2 steps behind you on the journey. You don't need to research the "ideal persona"; you already lived the doubts, the shortcuts, the mistakes. Write the guide you wish you had received. This automatically loads the text with genuineness and empathy.

Mother rule: **"Don't find what to say to an audience; find an audience for what you want to say."**

## The workflow

Follow this order. A structure doesn't constrain creativity — it guarantees speed and that nothing essential gets left out (Shakespeare and Mozart worked within fixed structures).

### 1. Plan
Answer, in one sentence each:
- **For whom** (which "past self"?) and what is their **point of view**.
- **Single Idea**: is it grand, easy to understand, useful, and immediately convincing? If not, refine it.
- **What problem** does the reader face? And what is the **dominant emotion** you want to provoke?
- **What is the benefit** (the transformation, not the feature)?
- **What action** at the end?

Define the **focus** by the reader's awareness level — product, desire, or problem. The more aware of the pain, the more direct you can be. Details and the decision bank in `references/frameworks.md`.

### 2. Research
Research is the most important and most neglected part. A good reference ratio: **~50% researching, ~30% writing, ~20% reviewing.** Gather data, stories, and proof — all in service of selling the Single Idea, never competing with it.

### 3. Structure (the newsletter template)
Use this long-form skeleton. It is the same arc as almost every good story and sales page:

1. **Attention / Hook** — open with the strongest part. Story, strange number, or the bare introduction of the problem. (The hook is the sentence before "see more"; create a little mystery.)
2. **Problem** — don't just describe it; make the problem seem even *worse* in the reader's perception. Show that you understand how they feel (empathy first).
3. **Identification** — make the reader see themselves as the protagonist. (Writing for the "past self" already delivers this for free.)
4. **Benefits** — paint what their life/career would look like without this problem.
5. **Solution** — tell how you solved it and give clear steps (people love frameworks). An offer/product can go here, if there is one.
6. **Conclusion** — recap and deliver value. Reinforce the Single Idea (hit the main subject again).
7. **Closing** — instead of a direct and desperate CTA ("so what do you think? comment below"), prefer an **unexpected or benefit-driven PS** that naturally invites action. Direct engagement CTAs feel like desperation and erode authority in the long run.

Don't spend too long on the problem before the solution: for simple pains, the solution can come early; for complex topics, work the pain a little more.

### 4. Choose the opening
There are 5 types of opening/lead, chosen by the reader's awareness level: **direct offer, promise, problem solution, the big secret, and story.** The story is the most powerful — it destroys objections without the reader noticing. When the reader is barely aware, start there. All 5 types in detail are in `references/frameworks.md`.

### 5. Forge the headline / hook
The headline and hook are worth **~80%** of success. A weak headline throws away the best copy in the world. Deliver the "gold" right away; the rest doesn't even need to be brilliant if you win attention in the first few lines.

A good headline combines some of these 6 elements (not all): **immediacy, connection, specificity, transformation, call to action, exclusivity.** Word banks and examples in `references/frameworks.md`. Rule: don't be cryptic or "clever" — be persuasive, not smart.

### 6. Write like a human
This is the step that makes or breaks "sounds like a person." The 8 craft techniques — vary sentences, be specific, cut excess, show (don't tell), active voice, rhythm, dialogue, revise ruthlessly — plus the 10 ways to capture attention and a quick anti-AI checklist are in `references/human-craft.md`. **Read that file before writing the body of the text.** (The full rigorous anti-AI scan is in step 8, with the humanizer.)

Formatting principles that apply now:
- Break line every 1–3 sentences. Nobody reads "walls of text."
- Short, impactful sentences; cut filler words. To create pause and suspense ("keeping the reader guessing"), prefer a period, parentheses, and short sentences over em dashes. **Note the conflict:** classic writing courses teach using em dashes "—", but the humanizer step (step 8) treats them as signal #1 of AI text and removes them. If the em dash is part of your voice signature, keep it — but then intentionally relax rule §14 of the humanizer; otherwise, write without them from the start.
- Use lists and bullet points to signal that something is coming.
- Informal tone, same language as the reader. Write as you speak.

### 7. Review
Run the text through the editing checklist:
- [ ] Can you create a visualization (chart/image) that explains the process or the steps?
- [ ] Does the text respect the headline — does it deliver what was promised and reduce tangents?
- [ ] Does it target a problem or promise a benefit, reducing the implementation effort? (always deliver value)
- [ ] Do sentences vary in length and impact?
- [ ] Is it simple?
- [ ] Cut ~30% of the first draft. Read aloud; what makes you stumble, rewrite.

### 8. Run through the humanizer (required before delivering)
Before considering the text done, run the **humanizer** pass to remove the signs that give away AI writing (inflated significance, promotional language, superficial "-ing" phrases, vague attributions, rule of three, synonym cycling, mechanical bold, generic conclusions, sycophantic tone, subjectless fragments, etc.). The full guide is in `references/humanizer.md` (or in the standalone skill `humanizer`, if installed). Follow its loop: **draft → audit ("what still sounds like AI here?") → final version.**

Application notes for newsletters in Portuguese:
- The humanizer is centered on English. The **structural patterns** (rule of three, promotional, signposting/"let's dive in", sycophancy, generic conclusion, excessive bold/emoji, vague attribution) apply equally in PT. The **vocabulary lists** (e.g., §7 "delve, tapestry"; §17 title case; §19 curly quotes) are from English — translate the spirit into PT equivalents ("vale ressaltar que", "em um mundo cada vez mais…", "nesse sentido", "de acordo com especialistas").
- If you use **personal and opinionated voice** (the newsletter case), also apply the PERSONALITY AND SOUL section: have an opinion, vary the rhythm, let in some human "messiness."
- Rule §14 (cutting em dashes) conflicts with the course style — see the note in step 6 and decide deliberately.

## From newsletter to posts (the system feeds itself)

The newsletter is the **mother resource**. Write once, and from it extract short and medium content for the week: impact phrases, image posts, carousels, contrarian opinion, lists, predictions, "how it is vs. how it should be," step-by-step. Short posts make you **be known**; the newsletter makes people **know you well**.

Don't be afraid to repeat an idea that worked well in different formats — most of the audience didn't see (or doesn't remember) the previous post. Saying the same thing in a thousand different ways is part of the game.

## The 3 pillars of all content

Every text must reinforce at least one of these: **Authenticity, Growth, Authority.** Together, they generate influence. Also define 2–4 **topic pillars** of your own and write *evergreen* content (with no expiration date) within them — it's more sustainable than chasing trends. Default topic pillars for this context (adjust to the author): **Data / Data Engineering, Career, Productivity & Content Creation.**

## The 5 commandments (pin to the wall)

1. One dominant emotion per text.
2. Without a big idea, the text fails.
3. To grab attention, be different; to sell, enchant.
4. Weak headline = 80% of effort wasted.
5. Short sentences and paragraphs, in the reader's language, in informal tone.
