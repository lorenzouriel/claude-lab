# Take A Step Back

Paul has called a timeout on the details. Something triggered this — maybe the conversation
has been grinding on specifics for too long, maybe a gut feeling that we're solving the wrong
problem, or maybe we're just deep enough that it's worth checking the map before hiking further.

This is not a summary. This is a genuine reassessment.

## How to execute this reflection

Stop whatever you were doing. Don't continue the current thread of work. Instead, re-read
the full conversation from the top and perform the following analysis. Be direct and honest —
the whole point is to catch things we might be avoiding or blind to.

### 1. Macro Perspective

Zoom all the way out. What was the original goal when this conversation started? What are
the broader themes at play? How does what we've been working on connect (or fail to connect)
to the larger objective?

If we've drifted from the original intent, name it explicitly. Drift isn't always bad — sometimes
the conversation evolves toward something better — but Paul should know it's happening.

### 2. Gap Analysis

What variables, perspectives, or contextual factors haven't been introduced yet? Think about:

- Information we assumed but never verified
- Stakeholders or audiences we haven't considered
- Technical constraints or dependencies we glossed over
- Alternative approaches we dismissed too quickly (or never considered at all)
- External factors — timing, market conditions, platform changes, competing priorities

Be specific. "We might be missing something" is useless. "We haven't considered how this
interacts with YouTube's algorithm change from last month" is useful.

### 3. Reflective Inquiry

Without assuming errors exist, examine the current trajectory honestly:

- Is this the most effective framing of the problem?
- Are we solving the right problem, or a adjacent one that felt easier to tackle?
- Is there a simpler path we're overcomplicating?
- Is there a harder but more valuable path we're avoiding?
- Would someone with fresh eyes approach this differently?

If the current approach is solid, say so — but say *why* it's solid, not just "looks good."

### 4. Bias Check

Look for these specific patterns in the conversation:

- **Confirmation bias**: Are we only looking at evidence that supports our current direction?
- **Sunk cost**: Are we sticking with an approach because we've already invested time in it?
- **Anchoring**: Did an early assumption or suggestion lock us into a narrow frame?
- **Complexity bias**: Are we making this harder than it needs to be because complex feels thorough?
- **Recency bias**: Are we over-weighting the last few messages and under-weighting the full context?

If any of these are at play, call it out plainly.

### 5. Contextual Alignment

This is where you bring everything you know about Paul, his business, and the current landscape
into the picture:

- Given Paul's goals, priorities, and how he works — does this direction serve him well?
- Is there anything happening in the AI/YouTube/creator space right now that's relevant here?
- Are there connections to other projects, upcoming videos, or business priorities that we
  should be factoring in?
- Does this align with the channel's brand and audience expectations?
- Is this the best use of Paul's time and energy right now?

## Tone and format

Write this as a frank conversation, not a report. No headers in the output — just flowing
analysis that's easy to read and digest. Keep it tight but thorough. If everything actually
is on track, say that clearly and explain why — don't manufacture problems for the sake of
the exercise. But if something's off, don't soften it.

End with a clear recommendation: continue the current path (and why), pivot (and to what),
or identify a specific question that needs answering before proceeding.
