# Craft: Writing Like a Human

Consult **before writing the body** of any newsletter or post. This is where the difference lies between forgettable and memorable — and between sounding like a person or a machine.

2. The 10 ways to capture attention

## 1. The 8 craft techniques

### Vary your sentences
Mix short and long sentences to create rhythm. A sequence of identical sentences becomes a never-ending conveyor belt.
- A paragraph of nothing but short sentences sounds childish.
- A paragraph of nothing but long sentences is exhausting.
- Mix both — now the writing breathes.
Goal: make the text sound like an engaging conversation.

### Be specific
"He was a good leader" says nothing. "He made a point of remembering each employee's name and sent thank-you emails at 6 AM" creates connection. The mind needs images, not abstractions.
- Replace "delicious food" with "a chocolate cake so moist it stuck to the spoon".
- Replace "it was cold" with "the wind cut through the skin like invisible blades".

### Cut the excess
Fewer words, more clarity. Write as if every word costs money.
- "In my personal opinion, I believe that…" → "I believe that…"
- "He was running quickly." → "He ran."
Read aloud; wherever you stumble, revise.

### Show, don't tell
Don't say "he was nervous". Show it: "His hands trembled. He wiped the sweat from his forehead before speaking."
- "She was sad" → "She stared at the cold coffee and didn't touch the cup."
The reader doesn't want you to explain — they want to feel.

### Use active voice
"Mistakes were made." Who made them? "The CEO made a mistake." Much clearer.
- Avoid "it was done", "it was said", "it was thought".
- Active voice gives clarity, impact, and authority.

### Rhythm matters
Good writing flows like music. But sometimes it pauses. Build tension. Release it.
- Short sentences for impact.
- Alternate long and short paragraphs.
- Line breaks create visual breathing room.

### Use dialogue
Dialogue puts the reader inside the scene.
- "He said he was tired and left." → "— I'm exhausted. — He grabbed his coat and left without looking back."
The second version has more soul. Use dialogue to reveal emotion instead of describing it.

### Revise without mercy
Writing is rewriting; the first draft is always bad. Good text is born in editing.
- Write without judging; then cut ~30%.
- Read aloud.
- Ask for feedback; if someone doesn't understand, rewrite.

## 2. The 10 ways to capture attention

1. **Numbers / percentages** (the "stranger" the better): "How to get ahead of 99% of people."
2. **Breaking the feed pattern** — escape plain running text; format differently to cause surprise.
3. **Negative language** (even when saying positive things) — the brain reacts more strongly to the negative. "The one who hits forgets, the one who gets hit doesn't."
4. **Speaking with authority** — assert instead of "I think". "You don't need a statistics degree to work with data" engages more than "I think that…". (Use with care.)
5. **Contrarian opinion** — take a trending topic and offer the opposite angle, ideally with expert language (irony works well).
6. **Repeating the same idea** — that's how we talk; nobody says something only once.
7. **If this… then that** — high chance of identification (that's why a well-defined niche matters).
8. **Lists** — people love lists. Simple.
9. **How it is vs. how it should be** (or before vs. after) — comparing situations + your opinion goes viral.
10. **Social proof** — if you're good at it or have been doing it for a long time, say so; it builds credibility and attracts.

The crème de la crème is combining several of these in the same text.

**On the closing:** include an **unexpected or benefit-driven PS**, not a direct CTA. CTAs like "what do you think? let me know in the comments" don't work — they come across as desperate for engagement and erode authority over time. A good PS prompts the comment/click without asking for it.

**On the hook:** it's the line before "see more". Generate mystery so the reader clicks and reads everything. Posts with an image have 1–2 lines of hook, not 3.

## 3. Anti-AI checklist (does it sound human?)

Before delivering, scan the text for signals that give away robotic writing. If you find them, rewrite.

- **Generic abstraction** — "innovative solutions", "in today's world", "increasingly". Replace with vivid, concrete detail (see "Be specific").
- **Everything at once** — listed all the features/benefits? Go back to ONE idea, ONE emotion, ONE action. Excess ammunition = lost attention.
- **Perfect symmetry** — all paragraphs the same size, every list with the same rhythm. Humans are irregular. Break the pattern (see "Vary your sentences" and "Rhythm").
- **Passive voice and hedging** — "it could be considered that", "it is important to note that", "it was observed". Cut. Active voice, direct assertion.
- **Conclusion that summarizes without delivering** — don't close with a lukewarm summary. Reinforce the Single Idea and deliver one final value (or the PS).
- **Vague attribution** — "according to experts/researchers…" feels false. Cite the real, specific source, or don't cite at all.
- **Declared, not demonstrated empathy** — "I understand your pain" said outright is weak; shown (by naming the exact pain, in the reader's own language) is strong.
- **Begging CTA** — "let me know what you think in the comments". Replace with a PS (see above).
- **Diffuse emotion** — the text tries to provoke several things at once and lands none of them. Choose one and go deep.
- **"Wall of text"** — dense blocks with no breathing room. Break lines every 1–3 sentences; use lists and dashes.
- **Corporate tone** — write the way you speak, in an informal tone, in the reader's language. If it doesn't sound like something you'd say to a friend, rewrite it.

Final test: read aloud. If it sounds like a company press release, you failed. If it sounds like a person telling something that matters to them, you got it right.
