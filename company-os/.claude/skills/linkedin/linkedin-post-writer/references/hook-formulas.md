# Hook formulas

Ten hooks that performed in 2025-2026. Each entry has a skeleton (the structural
template to fill with the user's voice) and a worked example that already obeys
the humanizer rules: no em dashes, varied sentence length, a real number, a named
entity, and a beat of vulnerability where the formula allows.

Reference engagement numbers are carried over from the skill table. Treat them as
relative signal (which formats tend to outperform), not guarantees.

## How to use this file

1. Read the user's topic and angle.
2. Match it against the "Best for" line on each formula, or against the
   [picking a formula](#picking-a-formula) table at the bottom.
3. Copy the skeleton. Replace every `[bracket]` with the user's specifics.
4. The first line of the skeleton must land inside 210 characters. That is the
   hook. Everything after the fold has to earn the click the hook made.
5. Run the humanizer checklist before you show anything to the user.

Brackets like `[number]` are mandatory fills. Do not ship a post with a bracket
or a placeholder still in it.

## F1 — Platform Risk Anaphora

- **Reference engagement:** 4,240
- **Best for:** category or platform posts, product-as-fix
- **Why it works:** anaphora (the same opening words repeated down the post)
  builds rhythm and dread at the same time. Each repetition raises the stakes,
  then the turn offers relief. The product, if any, is the relief, not the pitch.

**Skeleton**

```
[Platform] won't tell you [uncomfortable truth 1].

[Platform] won't tell you [uncomfortable truth 2].

[Platform] won't tell you [uncomfortable truth 3].

It doesn't have to.

[The turn: the one thing that survives the platform's whims.]

[Concrete personal stake or moment when this bit you.]

[Optional single product mention, only if it is the natural conclusion.]

[One sharp closing line, then a genuine question.]
```

**Worked example**

```
Instagram won't tell you when your reach drops 40% overnight.

Instagram won't tell you which post triggered it.

Instagram won't tell you how to get it back.

It doesn't have to. You don't own the audience. They do.

In March I lost 11,000 monthly views in a week. No warning. No reason
I could find. The only thing that held was my email list, 1,900 people
who got my work in their inbox no matter what the feed decided that day.

Rent your reach. Own your list.

What did you build this year that a platform can't take back?
```

## F2 — R.I.P. Obituary

- **Reference engagement:** 3,822
- **Best for:** era-ending claims, industry pivots
- **Why it works:** declaring something dead is a status claim. People show up to
  argue or to mourn, and both drive the thread. The obituary frame gives you a
  built-in structure: dates, cause of death, what survives it.

**Skeleton**

```
R.I.P. [thing], [start year]-[2026].

Cause of death: [the real reason, stated plainly].

It was [one honest tribute to what it did well].

[2-3 short lines on what changed and when you felt it].

[What replaces it, named specifically.]

[A line admitting what you still don't know, then a question.]
```

**Worked example**

```
R.I.P. the 9-step nurture sequence, 2014-2026.

Cause of death: nobody reads email 4 of 9 anymore.

It was good to us. It built half the SaaS companies you know.

I ran one for three years. My open rates fell every quarter and I kept
blaming the subject lines. It wasn't the subject lines. It was the nine.

What replaced it for us: one useful email, sent when someone actually
raises a hand. Reply rates tripled. I should have killed the sequence in 2023.

Still figuring out what counts as raising a hand. How do you measure intent?
```

## F3 — Year-over-Year Pivot

- **Reference engagement:** 494 (3.74x lift)
- **Best for:** identity shifts, founder reflection
- **Why it works:** a clean then/now contrast lets the reader measure distance.
  It reads as earned change rather than a brag because the "then" is unflattering.

**Skeleton**

```
[Past year] me: [unflattering belief or habit].
[This year] me: [the corrected version].

The thing that changed: [the specific event or realization].

[2-4 lines unpacking what it cost to learn this.]

[One line you'd tell last year's version of yourself.]

[Question that invites others to share their own pivot.]
```

**Worked example**

```
2024 me: said yes to every podcast, panel, and "quick coffee."
2026 me: says yes to four things a quarter and means all four.

The thing that changed: I tracked where one month actually went. 31 calls.
Two of them mattered. The other 29 cost me the product launch I kept
promising people was "coming soon."

Saying yes felt like momentum. It was just noise wearing a calendar.

If I could tell 2024 me one thing: a full calendar is not a full life.

What did you stop saying yes to this year?
```

## F4 — Time-Anchor Confession

- **Reference engagement:** 1,519+
- **Best for:** vulnerability, voice reset, re-targeting your ideal reader
- **Why it works:** a precise time and place ("2:14 AM, the office kitchen")
  reads as memory, not marketing. Specificity is the tell of a true story, and
  the confession buys trust you can spend later in the post.

**Skeleton**

```
[Precise time], [precise place]. I [did the embarrassing or vulnerable thing].

[What was actually going on under the surface.]

[2-3 lines of the turn: what you saw differently afterward.]

[The lesson, stated without a bow on it.]

[A question that lets the reader admit their own version.]
```

**Worked example**

```
11:40 PM, my kitchen table, last Tuesday. I rewrote the same paragraph
14 times and then deleted the whole document.

I told myself I was being rigorous. I was hiding. As long as it wasn't
finished, nobody could tell me it was bad.

The next morning I shipped the ugly version in nine minutes. Three people
replied within the hour. One of them is now a client.

Perfectionism is just fear with a nicer vocabulary.

What's the thing you keep polishing so you never have to publish it?
```

## F5 — Self-Proving Meta

- **Reference engagement:** 1,082 (435 comments)
- **Best for:** commitment-based posts, tests run in public
- **Why it works:** the post is its own evidence. You announce a test, the rules,
  and the witnesses. Comments become participation, not applause, which is why
  the comment counts run high.

**Skeleton**

```
This post is [the test, stated plainly].

The rules: [2-3 concrete constraints anyone can verify].

[Why you're doing it in public instead of privately.]

[What you'll report back and when.]

[An invitation to witness or to run their own version.]
```

**Worked example**

```
This post is a bet. I'm going to publish one thing every weekday for 30 days,
even the bad ones, especially the bad ones.

The rules: no deleting, no editing after an hour, no skipping a day. If I miss
one, I owe this thread a screenshot of the empty calendar.

I'm doing it here because private streaks die quietly. Public ones cost something.

Day 30 I'll post the full data: reach, replies, and how many days I wanted to quit.

Want to run your own 30? Drop your start date and I'll check on you at day 15.
```

## F6 — Comment-Gate Lead Magnet

- **Reference engagement:** 717-3,008 (use sparingly)
- **Best for:** list building
- **Caution:** the algorithm caps reach on posts that route everyone to the
  comments, and audiences are tired of "comment GUIDE below." Use this once a
  month at most, and make the magnet genuinely worth the friction.

**Skeleton**

```
[The specific, finished asset you're giving away.]

[One line proving it took real work or has real value.]

[The soft gate: comment [word] and I'll send it.]

[One reason it helps them this week, not someday.]

[A genuine line, not "tag someone who needs this."]
```

**Worked example**

```
I turned three years of cold outreach into a 12-message sequence that booked
41 meetings last quarter.

It's not theory. Every line is one I actually sent, with the reply rates noted.

Comment "SEQUENCE" and I'll send the doc. No opt-in wall, no funnel.

If you're staring at an empty pipeline going into June, this is the head start.

I read every comment, so if your outreach is stuck, tell me where and I'll
point you to the message that fixes it.
```

## F7 — Odd-Precision Money Ledger

- **Reference engagement:** 1,755 (9.4x lift)
- **Best for:** founder build-logs, cost breakdowns
- **Why it works:** round numbers read as estimates. Odd ones ($4,317.62) read
  as receipts. The precision signals you actually looked, and money is the most
  legible proof of effort there is.

**Skeleton**

```
[What it actually cost to do the thing], to the cent.

[Itemized ledger, each line an oddly precise number with a one-line note.]

[The total, then the surprising line item.]

[What the number taught you that the plan didn't.]

[A question about other people's real costs.]
```

**Worked example**

```
Here's what my "free" side project actually cost in year one: $6,842.19.

- Domain and hosting: $214.88
- The Figma seat I forgot to cancel: $431.40 (constant: annual plan, billed monthly)
- Contractor for the logo I redrew myself anyway: $900.00
- Ads I ran for 11 days before quitting: $1,206.91
- Coffee shop "offices": $4,089.00

That last line is the one that stopped me cold. I spent more on lattes than
on the actual product. Four grand to avoid working from my own desk.

What's the line item in your build that you'd rather not add up?
```

## F8 — Paid-vs-Free Reversal

- **Reference engagement:** 550 (19.64x lift)
- **Best for:** giving away a framework
- **Why it works:** anchoring on a price people pay, then handing the thing over
  free, creates an instant value gap. The whole post has to deliver the framework
  in full, or the reversal reads as a tease.

**Skeleton**

```
People pay [real price] for [the thing]. Here it is, free.

[The framework, given in full. Numbered or labeled steps, no gatekeeping.]

[The one step everyone skips, and what it costs them.]

[Why you're giving it away instead of charging.]

[A question that invites people to pressure-test it.]
```

**Worked example**

```
Consultants charge $2,500 to audit a landing page. Here's the checklist they
use, free.

1. Can a stranger say what you sell in five seconds? Time it.
2. Is the main button above the fold on a phone? Most aren't.
3. Does the headline name the reader's problem, not your feature?
4. Is there one action, or four competing ones?
5. Proof near the button: a number, a name, a face.

The step everyone skips is the phone test. I audited 30 pages last month and
22 of them buried the button two thumb-scrolls down.

I give this away because the audit was never the hard part. Doing the rewrite is.

Run it on your own page right now. Which line did you fail?
```

## F9 — Curiosity-Gap Teaser

- **Reference engagement:** 306 (4.25x lift)
- **Best for:** emergent behavior, behind-the-scenes
- **Why it works:** an open loop pulls the reader past the fold. The rule is that
  you must close the loop inside the post. A gap you never fill trains people to
  stop clicking your hooks.

**Skeleton**

```
[The strange result, stated before the explanation.]

[One line that deepens the mystery instead of resolving it.]

[The reveal, in steps, ending on the part that surprised you.]

[The takeaway that only makes sense now that the loop is closed.]

[A question inviting others' weird results.]
```

**Worked example**

```
We changed one word in our signup button and conversions jumped 27%.

It wasn't the word you're guessing. It wasn't "free."

We changed "Start your trial" to "Start building." Same flow, same price,
same everything else. We A/B tested it for 16 days across 9,400 visitors
because nobody on the team believed the first result.

Turns out people don't want a trial. They want to be the kind of person
who builds things. The button finally spoke to that.

What's the smallest change that gave you a result you couldn't explain at first?
```

## F10 — Contrarian + Historical Receipts

- **Reference engagement:** 3,083
- **Best for:** sacred-cow takes, AI and tech cycles
- **Why it works:** a contrarian claim gets attention; historical receipts keep
  you from looking like a troll. The pattern is thesis, then "we've seen this
  before," then the evidence that earns the take.

**Skeleton**

```
[Contrarian thesis that contradicts the current consensus.]

Everyone said the same about [historical parallel]. They were [right/wrong].

[The receipts: 2-3 concrete historical or data points.]

[What the pattern predicts for the thing you're actually talking about.]

[A line owning the risk that you're wrong, then a question.]
```

**Worked example**

```
"AI will replace junior developers" is the most confidently wrong take of 2026.

Everyone said the same about compilers in the 1950s. Programmers would be
obsolete once machines wrote the machine code. The number of programmers
went up roughly 100x in the 40 years after.

Spreadsheets were going to end accounting jobs in 1983. US accountant
headcount has grown most years since. Photoshop was going to end photography.

Tools that lower the cost of a skill usually grow the field, because cheaper
output means more demand for it, not less.

I might be wrong this time. The jump feels different. But I've heard "this
time is different" before too. Which side of this are you betting on?
```

## Picking a formula

If the user didn't name one, suggest 2-3 from this table by matching their topic.
Show the reference engagement number next to each suggestion so they can choose
with the numbers in front of them.

| If the topic is about... | Lead with | Backup |
|---|---|---|
| Owning your audience, platform danger | F1 (4,240) | F10 (3,083) |
| Declaring an old method dead | F2 (3,822) | F10 (3,083) |
| Personal change, founder reflection | F3 (494) | F4 (1,519+) |
| A vulnerable story, resetting voice | F4 (1,519+) | F3 (494) |
| A public commitment or live experiment | F5 (1,082) | F9 (306) |
| Building an email list | F6 (717-3,008) | F8 (550) |
| Real costs, build-in-public numbers | F7 (1,755) | F8 (550) |
| Giving away a framework or template | F8 (550) | F6 (717-3,008) |
| A surprising result, behind-the-scenes | F9 (306) | F7 (1,755) |
| A spicy, against-consensus take | F10 (3,083) | F2 (3,822) |

Notes for selection:

- F6 is reach-capped. Don't lead with it for a reach goal; pair it with a
  stronger format the week before.
- F3 and F9 have lower raw engagement but strong multipliers, which means they
  punch above their reach. Good for smaller accounts.
- F1, F2, and F10 are the high-ceiling formats. They also carry the most risk of
  reading as bait, so they lean hardest on the humanizer pass and on real stakes.
